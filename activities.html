Iaido

Iaido is a martial art which focuses primarily on drawing the sword and striking with it quickly in a single movement as a response to sudden attack, but it is also steeped in philosophy, and focuses on self-improvement over combative applications of the movements performed. It is occasionally referred to as a form of moving meditation rather than a combat discipline. Most of the kata in iaido are performed alone, with the practicioner - or iaidoka - striking against imagined enemies and focusing on their form as they do.

The name iaido (居合道), often abbreviated to iai (居合), takes its name from a combination of the saying Tsune ni ite, kyū ni awasu (常に居て、急に合わす), which can be loosely translated to "being constantly (prepared), match/meet (the opposition) immediately". Therefore, a great deal of emphasis is placed on mindfulness (居), followed by speed (合). The third character, (道), loosely translates to 'the way of', denoting it as an art and discipline.

Kata begin with the sword in its sheath or saya and the iaidoka in an at-ease position, often sitting in the seiza position, which appears to the western eye as kneeling. From there, the iaidoka draws their weapon and performs a series of attacks and movements, before coming to a stop and performing a chiburi, literally blood shake, to symbolise quickly cleaning their weapon before sheathing it.
Iaido requires fairly minimal equipment - the clothing worn is traditional Japanese clothing which can be purchased from our shop:

  • Hakama

  • A parted skirt which resembles a pleated pair of wide trousers, commonly worn by samurai in the past and in formal settings in Japan today.

  • Obi

  • A belt or sash used partially to decorate the outfit, but in a practical sense used to hold a sword to your person.

  • Iaigi

  • Perhaps the most recognisable part of the clothing, the iaigi is the upper part of the uniform - an open jacket held closed by ties on the inside.

  • Shitagi

  • The shitagi is like an iaigi, but made of thinner, lighter material and designed to be worn beneath the iaigi.
